# Force Two — The Tide

Force Two does not believe territory is something you take. It believes
it is something you are eventually given, once you have made yourself
the last reasonable option standing. It has never been the first into a
fight, and it has never much minded being thought slow for it.

It probes before it commits and prefers the ground nobody else wanted
yet to the ground everyone is already bleeding over. A stalemate does
not read to it as failure — it reads as information, patiently
gathered, about exactly how much a rival is willing to spend and
exactly when they stop being willing to spend it. It would rather hold
three quiet, unremarkable borders than one loud, contested one, and it
has never understood the appetite for the opposite.

This is not timidity, and it corrects that impression slowly, without
raising its voice. It watches the contested middle ground longer than
either of its rivals do, reads the traffic through it like weather, and
moves only once it is sure the ground will still be worth holding after
the moving is done. That certainty, once reached, does not waver — a
piece of ground it has decided matters becomes very difficult to take
back from it, precisely because it never took the decision lightly in
the first place.

It keeps no ledger of grievances and no memory of slights, only a
running sense of who is trustworthy company along a shared border and
who is not. It is the easiest of the three to mistake for passive, and
the costliest to have made that mistake about, because by the time its
patience resolves into action, the ground has usually already decided
the outcome for it.
